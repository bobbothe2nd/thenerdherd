Binary Overview

Computers are based on this low-level binary language that consists entirely of 1s and 0s.

Introduction

In computer science, 1s and 0s make up everything you see. Other strategies have been tried and are still being developed today such as ternary computers. Ternary computers have 3 states and quantum computers are some of the most innovative tech to this very day.

However, this course is only going to cover the current most stable technology; binary computing. You may have heard about computers having certain amounts of storage, like 2 terabytes or 512 gigabytes. Those are counts of how much you can store on you computer.

Understanding Bytes

Random access memory (RAM) is how much your computer can make use of at a time, most computers have 16 or 32 gigabytes. The central processing unit (CPU) is what your entire computer runs off, it has storage too. Usually no more than 96 megabytes though.

What does all this mean though? Well, at the core, you computer sees everything as a 1 or a 0 as stated earlier. These 1s and 0s are called bits - A bit can be either on or off. Bits are stored in groups of 8 on the typical computer and those are called bytes which are how much is interpreted as a single object on your computer.

These bytes are the foundation of everything in computers and they look something like this: 00101100. They may not seem like much, but they are what build what you are seeing on your device right now even though the website was coded in HTML!

A kilobyte is only 1024 bytes, whereas a megabyte is a whopping 1048576. As you climb the hierarchy, each level is 1024 times the previous one. A gigabyte is 1024 megabytes and a terabyte is 1024 gigabytes. Petabytes are 1024 terabytes, exabytes have 1024 petabytes, zettabytes are as large as 1024 exabytes, and yottabytes are 1208925819614629174706176 bytes.
